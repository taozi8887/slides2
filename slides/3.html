Mustard Gas

Development and Use

Synthesized in the 19th century by Frederick Guthrie, developed further by Viktor Meyer
Weaponized in early 20th century
First used by German forces in WWI, later by both sides
Introduced in 1917 at the Battle of Ypres, Belgium

Deployment in Warfare

Launched via artillery shells containing liquid sulfur mustard
Vaporized upon impact, spreading across wide areas
Gas masks ineffective; mustard gas affected skin and eyes
Caused approximately 120,000 casualties by war's end

Characteristics of Mustard Gas

Delayed effects; symptoms appear hours after exposure
Caused painful, slow deterioration – physical agony and mental distress
Aimed to incapacitate, disrupt morale, and create lasting fear

Mustard Gas

Impact in Trench Warfare

Effective in confined trenches with limited ventilation
Drifted over trenches, clung to mud, walls, and gear
Continued risk of exposure long after initial attacks

Physical and Psychological Harm

Physical effects: Delayed but severe symptoms – blisters, respiratory distress, blindness
Psychological effects: Instilled terror; incapacitated more than killed
Ethical Concerns

Use of chemical warfare questioned on ethical grounds
Viewed as a tool of psychological warfare

Underground Mining

Purpose: Used as a destructive tactic in WWI

Method: Extensive tunnels dug beneath enemy lines to plant mines under fortifications, trenches, or supply routes

Counter Mining: Both sides used traps to counteract enemy mines

Notable Example: Battle of Messines (1917), Western Front

British detonated explosive mines, causing significant damage to German forces

Shifted the tide of war by inhibiting German advancements

Psychological Impact: Soldiers feared tunneling due to constant risk of explosion
